Grace Hopper

“A ship in port is safe, but that's not what ships are built for.” --Grace Hopper

Grace Murray Hopper (biography.com) was a leader in the field of computer science and mathematics, as well as a rear admiral in the U.S. Navy. She attended Vassar College as a young adult and later earned a Ph.D. in mathematics at Yale -- a very difficult task for women at the time. However, her most inspiring and groundbreaking accomplishments involve making code accessible to other people.

It wasn’t an easy task at the time -- user interface didn’t exist (digitaltrends.com). Computers were very primitive, and so were people’s views regarding them. At one point, Hopper described how many people were frustrated by her insistence on communicating with computers using English rather than complex code. Hopper’s persistence and grit carried her through, nevertheless, and the world’s first working compiler was born. Entirely driven by the spirit of inclusivity, Hopper knew she wanted to ensure that the process of writing code wouldn’t be unfathomable to most people -- and it paid off (space.com).

Although Hopper was raised during a very eventful and tumultuous period in women’s rights history, she realized her dream of spreading the use of code through the creation of useful tools such as compilers. Now, she’s permanently embedded in history as one of the most prominent and brilliant women in technology and science, she has earned the Presidential Medal of Freedom, and she earned the rank rear admiral during her career with the Navy.

Brit Marling

“Nothing seemed as scary as waking up at 40 and realizing that I had not lived a very courageous life.” --Brit Marling

The media industry is a known battleground for women’s rights and equality, and Brit Marling is one of its leaders, seizing control of her career. In Highschool she described herself as an overachieving nerd, who wanted to be class president and head of the newspaper (wmagazine.com). After graduating as valedictorian (interviewmagazine.com) with a degree in economics at Georgetown University, Brit Marling interned at Goldman Sachs, prior to turning down a full time job offer. Instead, she decided to move to Hollywood to make movies with more realistic and honest representations of women than what was already present. She refused small roles in films offered to her with the goal of writing her own films. She says,"I think about things my little sister has to deal with, the struggle it is to be a young girl in this world, and it makes me determined to play interesting women. I have a hard time being a part of, or even thinking about, an image system that has oppressed women.” (theguardian.com)

Marling’s films have featured at Sundance, and she’s currently working on a netflix show called, “The OA.” Writing and directing her own work also allows her to cast interesting and unique women in different roles, who may not have received the role otherwise. Phyllis Smith, for example, had a role tailored for her in the show. Marling particularly enjoys writing science fiction, as she appreciates the opportunity it presents to portray metaphors about life in a manner that’s easy to consume (interviewmagazine.com).

Marling still believes there’s a lot to be done, and feels that the gender gap can sometimes be heartbreaking -- yet, she continues to invest herself in her work and pour her heart and soul into her art.

Fe Del Mundo

“I feel that if you give the world the best that you can, the best will always come back to you.” - Fe del Mundo

Fe del Mundo (1911-2011) was a Filipino pediatrician and the first woman to enroll at Harvard Medical School, 13 years before it began accepting women. Harvard officials didn’t realize she was a woman until she arrived there in 1936 to begin her studies, but they allowed her to stay because of her incredible academic record. (amazingwomeninhistory.com) For del Mundo, this was nothing new, since she had been defying odds throughout her life.

Deeply affected by the loss of four of her seven siblings to various disease, she decided to study pediatrics. Despite extensive postgraduate study in the United States, even on the brink of World War II, del Mundo declined invitations to work in the US and returned to the Philippines to apply her studies at home.

During World War II, del Mundo was commended for expanding the women’s and children’s ward, saving hundreds of lives. She also designed a laundry basket incubator for rural families, ran immunization campaigns, and taught medicine for over 30 years. But in 1957, del Mundo accomplished her ultimate dream: opening up a children’s hospital. She had to sell her own house to keep it afloat, but as she said, “the satisfaction derived from guiding and serving children is worth much more than a check.” ( gmanetwork.com)

With her talent, she could have made millions in America, but Fe del Mundo never lost sight of her roots. Even in the face of tragedy and danger, she continued serving her people instead of prioritizing personal wealth and power. And in the end, she changed the world in ways that really mattered.

Amal Clooney

"If you are a lawyer, and you want to take on easier cases, you can prosecute traffic violations or something. You’d have a very high rate of success, and you probably could sleep more easily at night. But that’s not what drives me. I want to work on cases that I feel the most passionate about." --Amal Clooney

Would you be surprised to know that when one searches up “Amal Clooney,” all the primary articles presented by Google are about her style and looks? While Amal Clooney does rock her personal style, she is still so much more than that. She currently works as a barrister at Doughty Street Chambers, and specializes in public international law and human rights. Previously, she worked as a defense attorney at Sullivan & Cromwell in New York, where she helped to represent former Enron executives and other corporate clients. In her time, Clooney has represented several high-profile individuals such as Julian Assange, and former Ukrainian Prime Minister Yulia Tymoshenko. Additionally, she also fought for the Elgin Marbles to be returned to Greece, and worked to free three Al-Jazeera journalists imprisoned in Egypt. ( independent.co.uk)

The media often reports her accomplishments with infatuation over her looks. Her greatest achievements are downplayed when people see her simply doing her job as a bonus to looking nice, instead of the other way around. (time.com) Regardless, Amal Clooney works hard to defend the marginalized communities she serves and does so whether or not she is styling a nice hairdo or not. She recently told BBC News, “There is lots of my work that takes place behind closed doors that is not ever seen. I think if there are more people who now understand what’s happening about the Yazidis and ISIS, and if there can be some action that results from that, that can help those clients, then I think it’s a really good thing to give that case the extra publicity that it may get.” Clooney is aware of this power she has over the media with her looks, and she tries to make the best of an unfortunate situation by trying to bring attention to what really matters.

Furthermore, Clooney is well known in the field of international law for advocating on behalf of exploited groups. “If you are a lawyer and you want to take on easier cases, you can prosecute traffic violations or something. You’d have a very high rate of success, and you probably could sleep more easily at night,” she said in January 2016. “But that’s not what drives me. I want to work on cases that I feel the most passionate about.”
